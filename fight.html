FIGHT

In Kentucky, one of the gardener's biggest foes is humidity, especially in the Ohio Valley. Humidity is a breeding ground for fungal diseases like powdery mildew, blight, and anthracnose. The constant water content in the air during the summer makes these diseases spread even if it doesn't rain. The worst insects in the area include beetles, vine borers, squash bugs, and tomato hornworms. Many of the most harmful insects and diseases begin showing up in June when the weather starts to get significantly warmer. Read on to learn more about some of Kentucky's worst offenders!

INSECTS & DISEASES

Cucumber Beetle
A cucumber beetle resembles a ladybug in size and appearance, but is easily distinguishable due to its bright yellow color. The adults can be found on cucurbits such as cucumbers, squash, watermelon, and a variety of other plants. They are notorious pests of agricultural crops. The insects live for about eight weeks, during which time both they feed on plants. Adults will attack the tender young growth of stems and leaves and the buds and petals on mature specimens. They also carry and spread bacterial wilt, a disease that can kill entire plants in a matter of weeks. Eradication efforts include clearing cultivated areas of litter, debris, and infested plants. They are also attracted to the color yellow, so placing yellow sticky traps in the area will often capture hundreds of them and take them out of circulation in your garden.
Squash Bug
The squash bug is a major pest of squash and pumpkins and is found throughout North America. They emit an unpleasant odor when killed. The adult female lays two or three batches of about eighteen eggs, oval and bronze in colour, and are deposited on the underside of the leaves of the host plant. The eggs hatch after seven to nine days into nymphs and immediatley begin feeding on the plant where they emerged. They physically damage the xylem and leaves of the plant, which causes them to wilt, darken in colour and die. The heavier the infestation, the greater the damage to the plant. They are large bugs and can often be manually removed and dropped into a bucket of soapy water and left to drown. If not controlled, they will multiply by the hundreds and completely decimate entire crops of vegetables.
Squash Vine Borer
The squash vine borer is a species of moth that is often mistaken for a bee or wasp because of its movements, and its bright orange hind leg scales. The females typically lay their eggs at the base of leaf stalks, and the caterpillars develop and feed inside the stalk, eventually killing the leaf. They soon migrate to the main stem, and with enough feeding damage to the stem, the entire plant may die. It attacks cultivated varieties of squash, zucchini, pumpkin, and acorn squash. The squash vine borer is native to North America, with some reports as far south as Brazil and Argentina. It lives in most temperate US states, except the Pacific coast. Southern states have two broods a year. A small utility knife and tweezers, or other fine tools, may be used to gently cut open the stem and try to extract the larva before it can kill the plant.
Tomato Hornworm
The tomato hornworm is a major pest in gardens; they get their name from a dark projection on their posterior and their use of tomatoes as host plants. Tomato hornworms are closely related to (and sometimes confused with) the tobacco hornworm Manduca Sexta. This confusion arises because caterpillars of both species have similar morphologies and feed on the foliage of various plants in the tomato and tobacco families. Because of this, the plant on which the caterpillar is found does not indicate its species. They commonly feed on tomato, eggplant, pepper, tobacco, moonflowers and potato. Caterpillars can often be found on plants during the day and are generally high up in the leaves. They are very large insects, often over 1 inch in length, and can be manually removed from plants with ease and disposed of.
Japanese Beetle
The Japanese beetle is a species of scarab beetle. The adult has an iridescent copper-colored body and a green thorax and head. It is not very destructive in Japan (where it is controlled by natural predators), but in North America and some regions of Europe it is a noted pest to roughly 300 species of plants, including rose bushes, grapes, hops, canna, crape myrtles, birch trees, linden trees, and others. The adult beetles damage plants by skeletonizing the foliage (consuming only the material between a leaf's veins) as well as feeding on a plant's fruit. The subterranean larvae feed on the roots of grasses. Owing to its destructive nature, traps have been invented specifically to target Japanese beetles. These comprise a pair of crossed walls with a bag or plastic container underneath and are baited with floral scent, pheromone, or both. The bugs crawl inside, get trapped and die.
Fusarium Wilt
Fusarium wilt is a common vascular wilt fungal disease. The fungal pathogen Fusarium Oxysporum affects a wide variety of plants of any age. Tomato, tobacco, legumes, cucurbits, sweet potatoes and banana are a few of the most susceptible plants, but it also infects other herbaceous plants. The fungus generally produces symptoms such as wilting, chlorosis, necrosis, premature leaf drop, browning of the vascular system, stunting and damping-off. It starts out looking like vein clearing on the younger leaves and drooping of the older lower leaves, followed by stunting, yellowing of the lower leaves, defoliation, and plant death. Once the fungus has appeared, it is generally too late to save the plant. Preventative steps should be taken to prevent its appearance, such as the application of natural fungicides like Copper and Mancozeb.
Tomato Blight
Blight is a rapid and complete chlorosis, browning, then death of plant tissues such as leaves, branches, twigs, flowers, or fruit. When blights have been particularly vast and consequential in their effects, they have become named historical events, such as the 19th Century Potato Blight, also known as the Great Famine of Ireland. Also notable is the near extinction of the Bermuda cedar during the 1940s and 1950s in the event described as The Cedar Blight. In tomatoes, blight often appears from the lower leaves of the plant and works its way upward, first manifesting as yellowing leaves and then appearing as black spots on the stems and leaves and eventually on the fruit itself. There is no cure for blight; any plant beleived to be suffering from the disease should be removed from the garden and destroyed as quickly as possible.
Powdery Mildew
Powdery mildew is a fungal disease that affects a wide range of plants. The disease is caused by many different species of fungi and is one of the easier plant diseases to identify, as its symptoms are quite distinctive. Infected plants display white powdery spots on the leaves and stems. The lower leaves are the most affected, but the mildew can appear on any above-ground part of the plant. As the disease progresses, the spots get denser as large numbers of spores are formed, and the mildew may spread up and down the length of the entire plant. Powdery mildew grows well in environments with high humidity and moderate temperatures, such as Kentucky. The disease is also known to cause harm to agricultural and horticultural practices in a greenhouse setting. Preventative maintenance is the key: apply natural fungicides such as Copper and Mancozeb before the disease makes an appearance.
Downy Mildew
Despite its name, Downy Mildew is not actually mildew, but rather microbes that are parasites of plants. In commercial agriculture, they are a particular problem for growers of grapes and any vegetables that grow on vines. Yield loss associated with downy mildew is usually related to soft rots that occur after plant canopies collapse and sunburn occurs on fruit. Initial symptoms include large, angular or blocky, yellow areas visible on the upper surface that expand rapidly and turn brown. In disease-favorable conditions (cool nights with long dew periods), downy mildew will spread rapidly, destroying all leaf tissue. One way to control the spread is to eliminate moisture and humidity around the impacted plants. You can achieve this by watering from below, such as with a drip system, and improving air circulation through selective pruning.
Leaf Spot
A leaf spot is a discolored, diseased area of a leaf that is caused by fungal, bacterial or viral plant diseases. These discoloured spots or lesions often have a center of necrosis (cell death). Prolonged wet and humid conditions promote leaf spot disease and most pathogens are spread by wind, splashing rain or irrigation that carry the disease to other leaves. Spots can vary in size, shapes and colors depending on the age and type of the cause or pathogen. Plants, shrubs and trees are weakened by the spots on the leaves as they reduce the ability to complete photosynthesis. Fungicides such as Zineb, Chlorothalonil and Captan, can be used to treat the disease. If the spots appear later in the season, they can usually be ignored if present on deciduous plants since the leaves will be dropped. Be sure to bag the leaves and remove them to prevent the disease from reappearing the following year.

GROW

Growing a garden in Kentucky can be a little challenging when compared to other parts of the United States that stay warmer all year round. Kentucky is in Hardiness Zone 6, an area known for mild climates with cold winters and humid summers. The state's growing season lasts from early spring to early fall, with the winter's last frost occurring in mid-March and the first frost falling in November. Few gardens can survive outside that window unless protected in a greenhouse. Take a look below to see some of the most popular vegetables that can be grown in Kentucky!

VEGETABLES & HERBS

Tomatoes

Virtually any type of tomato can be grown in Kentucky. There are hundreds of varieties to choose from, whether you plant your own seed or buy plants that have been locally grown. After planting them, amend the soil with bone meal and 10-10-10 fertilizer. In the absence of rain, water them at least twice a week. You'll also need to use some type of support, such as a cage or a stake, or they will eventually get so tall that they will collapse under their own weight. Never leave tomatoes lying on the ground or they are likely to become diseased or be stolen by neighborhood critters. A shifty squirrel will gladly make anything you leave lying around disappear. Most tomatoes are ripe when they are red, but depending on the varitey, they could be orange, yellow, or even purple. Be sure to research your variety so you don't pick it too soon.

Peppers

Peppers are fairly easy to grow. There are not very many pests or animals that eat the leaves or the peppers themselves. Popular varieties are Bell, Jalapeño, Serrano, and Habañero. Your plants will need plenty of sun if you want them to ripen quickly. Peppers grown in shade will ripen very slowly, or sometimes not at all. They come in a variety of colors, but the color typically doesn't have much bearing on the flavor. Depending on personal taste, you can grow peppers that range from sweet all the way to face-melting hot. The hotness of peppers is measured using the Scoville Scale, a numerical system that assigns numbers to peppers to give an indication of how hot they are. It ranges from 0 for bell peppers all the way into the millions for peppers like the Carolina Reaper. Only a crazy person would ever eat one of those!

Onions

Onion bulbs should be planted about one inch beneath the soil surface. Lightly pack the dirt over top of them, but do not smash it flat or the bulb may have difficulty sprouting and reaching the surface. Like peppers, most animals and pests don't like the taste of onions and tend to leave them alone. They can be harvested at almost any stage. Once the onion sprouts and grows a stalk several inches high, it can be picked as a "green onion" and eaten plain or chopped up for use in meals. As the onions grow larger, they become hotter and more pungent. Once the green stalks wilt and fall over, your onion is as big as it is going to get and is ready to be picked. Depending on the variety, they can grow anywhere from the size of a golf ball to the size of a grapefruit. Onions can tolerate dry conditions, but water them at least twice a week to maximize growth potential.

Garlic

Garlic is one of the simplest plants to grow. There are very few pests that attack it and most animals hate the smell of it and leave it alone. You can plant garlic in the spring for a summer harvest, but for best results, plant it in the last week of October and let it grow all the way until June of the following year. The cold won't kill it and will help make it hardier. Hardneck garlic bulbs will grow scapes, which are green stalks that curl and eventually grow a flower on the end. Once these scapes are about a foot long, you can cut them off and eat them. Removing the scape forces the bulb to stop putting effort into growing a flower and reproducing and makes it put all its effort into bulb growth, nearly doubling the size of the bulb in most cases. Wait about another month after scape removal and your garlic will be ready to pick.

Watermelon

Watermelon is not one of the easiest fruits to grow, but it is one of the most delicious. Depending on the variety, watermelons can be anywhere from the size of a bowling ball on up into the hundreds of pounds. In Kentucky, the most popular melons are the Crimson Sweet and the Sugar Baby. Watermelons can be very difficult to grow without using some type of fungicide, such as copper or Mancozeb. Humidity tends to cause downy mildew to appear and kill the plants before they can fully ripen. If you apply fungicide every two weeks during the growing season and provide a lot of water, you will have beautiful melons ready to be picked by the beginning of August. Beware of downy mildew, the main nemesis of the watermelon. This fungus can easily destroy your entire crop.

Cucumbers

Cucumbers can grow on the ground, but they are for more successful when allowed to climb upward on a trellis, allowing for airflow that will help prevent disease. Pollinators such as bees and ants are crucial to the plants, so be sure to avoid insecticides wherever possible. Most cucumbers are ready to be picked when they are 8 inches long. If you leave them on the vine too long, they will turn yellow. There are certain varieties of cucumbers that have been bred specifically for pickling. These are usually smaller fruits about 4-6 inches long. Cucumber vines can grow upwards of ten feet in length unless you opt for the "Bush" variety, which literally grows into a small bush full of cucumbers. Whichever type you choose, cucumbers are best picked when on the smaller size because they will become bitter as they grow larger.

Zucchini

There are dozens of types of squash that can be grown in Kentucky, but the most popular is definitely the zucchini. Zucchini grows quickly and easily when given a high phosphate fertilizer that will help the vegetables develop. It is important to trim back the plant if it becomes too thick, removing overlapping leaves and allowing more airflow between the plants to prevent the appearance of powdery mildew. Zucchini grows as a small bush and will have multiple fruit growing from the stem at one time. Zucchini is at its best when picked when it's about 8-12 inches long. As they grow larger, the skin gets tougher and starts to taste a little bitter. Slice it up and put it on the grill with a little olive oil, salt and pepper for a fantastic summer treat. Watch out for bees that get stuck in the flower blossoms when they close... sometimes you have to help them escape!

Potatoes

Potatoes should be planted a few inches beneath the soil. As they grow, you will begin to see the soil crack and rise up. It is important to continue to add additional soil at the base of the plants in order to prevent any of the potatoes from breaking through the surface. Eventually, you will end up with a small mound of dirt that could be 8-12 inches in height. If they are exposed to the sunlight, potatoes will begin to turn green and can actually make you sick if you eat them. As the stalks grow above ground, they will eventually develop small flowers. Pinch these flowers off and discard them to make the potatoes put more of their energy into growing the tuber underground instead of trying to reproduce above ground. When potatoes are picked, put them in a box in a cool, dark place. They should last up to 3 weeks. Don't wash them or they will begin to rot much sooner.

Asparagus

Asparagus should be planted about 6 inches deep. As the plant begins to grow, you must not harvest it for at least 3 years or you run the risk of killing the plant. Just let it grow and then cut it all down at the end of the year. At the beginning of the third year, you can begin harvesting spears for about 6 weeks. Then let the plant continue to grow. Never harvest all season long or there will be nothing left to grow back the next year. As it grows, the plant essentially turns into a fern and can grow nearly eight feet tall. Some varieties produce berries that attract birds to the area. Unless you plan to use the berries, the birds are harmless. At the end of the year, cut the plant all the way down to the ground to allow it time to overwinter and come back again even stronger the following year.

Herbs

Herbs are abundant in Kentucky. Rosemary, thyme, basil, oregano, sage, mint... they all have wonderful aromas and can add a lot of flavor to any home cooked meal. Nearly all herbs thrive in a nice, sunny spot and need to be watered 2-3 times a week during dry spells. When you trim them, never remove more than 2/3 of the plant or you may damage its ability to return the next year. When trimming, you can allow any excess to dry in the sun or you can dry it in a food dehydrator. Virtually any herb can be dried and then crushed into flakes or pulverized into poweder in a food processor. Then you have your very own dried seasonings that are much more flavorful than anything you'd ever buy at the store. Dried herbs will last for years if stored in a cool, dry food pantry.
